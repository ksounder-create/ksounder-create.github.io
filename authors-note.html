Author’s Note: The Recovery of a Lost Central Principle What is presented on this site is not the invention of a new belief about life. It is the recovery of a principle that was always there, now supported by an extraordinary body of experimental evidence. Modern molecular biology and the Indic Knowledge System arrived at the same conclusion by very different paths: that Nature operates through a single, pervasive law, and that complexity is its expression rather than its contradiction. In biology, this principle was buried under decades of fragmented data and inherited assumptions. In philosophy, it was obscured by layers of symbolism and ritual. Each tradition preserved a shadow of the same truth, and each was incomplete on its own. Together, they made it possible to recover the law that governs life. For decades, biology appeared to be making steady progress. New genes, new pathways, new drugs, and new technologies appeared every year. Yet beneath this surface activity, something fundamental was missing. Despite immense effort, no single molecule was ever fully defined in terms of its necessary behavior. No cellular process was ever mechanistically closed. No disease was ever explained without ambiguity. Everything remained conditional, context-dependent, and statistical. This was not because the experiments were wrong. It was because the foundational assumptions were. Modern molecular biology was built on conceptual frameworks — especially those embedded in the Central Dogma — that were not specific enough to define what any molecule truly is or how it must behave. Experimental methods produced data, but they did not produce molecular definitions. As a result, biology developed an unusually high tolerance for ambiguity, contradiction, and paradox. I began this work from a simple premise: life cannot be sustained without logic, science, and governing rules. Yet my initial search for even one fully consistent, well-defined molecular fact in the literature failed. Everywhere I looked, the same pattern appeared — an almost total coherence of ambiguity, contradiction, and paradox across experimental results — the opposite of what a lawful system should produce. That failure forced a different question: not why biology is complex, but why it appears lawless. By abandoning inherited assumptions and demanding a framework with zero contradictions, a deeper pattern emerged — a single fundamental rule capable of generating extraordinary biological complexity. Halfway through this journey, I noticed something unexpected. The way biological knowledge had fragmented into thousands of pathways, exceptions, and special cases closely resembled a pattern long recognized in the Indic Knowledge System. In that tradition, a single unifying principle of Nature was expressed through many symbols, stories, and forms. Over time, those representations multiplied, and the underlying unity they were meant to convey became obscured. In biology, the same thing had happened in a different way. Enormous quantities of experimental data — generated in the hope of uncovering the central principle of life — ended up burying it beneath complexity. Paradoxes and exceptions were treated as intrinsic features of life, rather than as signs of a missing rule. The methodological lesson from the Indic tradition — that diversity must arise from unity, and complexity from a deeper, simpler law — proved invaluable when I returned to biological data. Instead of treating paradoxes as noise, I treated them as signals of an underlying rule. Instead of accepting exceptions, I demanded coherence. That way of thinking led directly to the discovery of governing biological rules — and to the realization that: Nature has one law; everything else is its expression. — Soundar Krish
